Basic notions in Statistics

Population

Population is the entire set of items from which data is drawn for a statistical study. It can be a group of individuals, a set of items etc. It constitutes the data pool for a study. The items of this set are denominated Statistical Units.

Statistical Unit

A statistical unit is the basic entity or element about which data is collected in a statistical survey or study. It represents the individual entity that is observed or measured, such as an individual or an item. The phenomenons that we can observe on each statistical unit are called characters or variables. A character is more general and abstract, while a variable is more precise. For example, if we consider a person as a statistical unit, we can take as a variable the weight of the person and define it in a quantitative way (for example 75 kg) or a qualitative way (for example low, medium, high).

Distribution

In Statistics, Distribution refers to how the values of a variable or dataset are spread out or arranged. It describes the frequency with which different values occur and can be represented in various ways, such as tables, charts, or mathematical functions. Types of Distribution

  • Frequency Distribution: Shows how often each value or range of values appears in a dataset. This can be represented using a histogram for quantitative data or a bar chart for qualitative data.
  • Probability Distribution: Defines the probability that a random variable will take on different values. Probability distributions can be discrete (like the roll of a die) or continuous (such as the normal distribution for quantitative data).
  • Normal Distribution: Also known as the “Gaussian distribution,” it is a symmetrical, bell-shaped distribution where values are concentrated around the mean. Many natural phenomena tend to follow a normal distribution.
Understanding the distribution of data is essential in statistical analysis because it provides insights into central tendency (such as mean and median), variability (such as variance and standard deviation), and shape (such as skewness and kurtosis). Knowing the distribution helps in making predictions, making decisions, and interpreting statistical results effectively.

Frequency

In statistics, the Frequency of an event is the number of times the observation has occurred/recorded in an experiment or study. Types of Frequency:

  • Absolute Frequency: It is simply the count of how many times a particular value or item appears without considering the relative size of the dataset.
  • Relative Frequency/Percentage: The relative frequency of an event is the absolute frequency normalized by the total number of events. The formula for relative frequency is given by: \[ f_{i} = \frac{n_{i}}{N} = \frac{n_{i}}{\sum _{j} n_{j}} \]

Notion of arithmetic mean

The arithmetic mean is an indicator of the central tendency that is calculated by summing all the values of the items in a set and then dividing it by the total number of the items. It's useful to understand the typical value within a dataset. Mathematically, the arithmetic mean is defined as: \[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n} \]

Floating Point Representation

Floating point representation is a method used in computers to represent real numbers, allowing for a wide range of values to be expressed with a finite number of bits. It mimics scientific notation, where numbers are expressed as a significant (or mantissa) and an exponent. The general format is:

value = sign × mantissa × 2exponent

In this representation:

  • The sign bit indicates whether the number is positive or negative.
  • The mantissa contains the significant digits of the number.
  • The exponent determines the scale or range of the number.

Problems Associated with Floating Point Representation

  • Catastrophic Cancellation:

    This occurs when subtracting two nearly equal numbers, leading to a significant loss of precision. For example, in the calculation a - b where a and b are close in value, the significant digits can cancel out, resulting in a less accurate result. This is particularly problematic in iterative calculations or algorithms that rely on small differences.

  • Overflow:

    Overflow happens when a computed value exceeds the maximum limit that can be represented within the allocated bits. This can lead to incorrect results, such as wrapping around to a negative number in two's complement representation or returning infinity.

  • Underflow:

    Underflow occurs when a value is too small to be represented in the floating point format, leading to values being rounded down to zero. This can cause issues in computations, particularly when small values are significant to the result.

  • Precision Loss:

    Floating point numbers have limited precision, typically around 7 to 15 decimal digits (depending on whether single or double precision is used). When performing arithmetic operations on large numbers or very small numbers, precision can be lost, affecting the accuracy of results.

  • Rounding Errors:

    Because many decimal fractions cannot be exactly represented in binary (e.g., 0.1), rounding occurs during conversions between formats. Accumulated rounding errors can lead to inaccuracies in long calculations.

  • Non-associativity:

    The result of floating point arithmetic can vary based on the order of operations due to the above errors. For example, (a + b) + c may yield a different result than a + (b + c), leading to inconsistent results.
