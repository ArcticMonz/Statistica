Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality, Independence, and Uncorrelation

The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is a fundamental result in linear algebra and analysis, particularly within inner product spaces. For vectors \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \) in an inner product space, the inequality states:

\[ |\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\| \]

where \( \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle \) is the inner product (or dot product in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)) of \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \), and \( \|\mathbf{a}\| \) and \( \|\mathbf{b}\| \) denote the magnitudes (norms) of \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \), respectively. Equality holds if and only if \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \) are linearly dependent, meaning one is a scalar multiple of the other.

Proof of the Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

  1. Define a function of a scalar \( t \):
    Consider a scalar \( t \) and the vector \( \mathbf{a} - t \mathbf{b} \). The norm squared of this vector is always non-negative:

    \[ \|\mathbf{a} - t \mathbf{b}\|^2 \geq 0. \]

  2. Expand the norm squared:
    Expanding \( \|\mathbf{a} - t \mathbf{b}\|^2 \) using the properties of the inner product:

    \[ \|\mathbf{a} - t \mathbf{b}\|^2 = \langle \mathbf{a} - t \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{a} - t \mathbf{b} \rangle. \]

    Applying the distributive property of the inner product, this becomes:

    \[ = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle - 2t \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle + t^2 \langle \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{b} \rangle. \]

    Using the notation \( \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle \) and \( \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 = \langle \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{b} \rangle \), we rewrite this as:

    \[ \|\mathbf{a} - t \mathbf{b}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 - 2t \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle + t^2 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2. \]

  3. View as a quadratic inequality in \( t \):
    Since \( \|\mathbf{a} - t \mathbf{b}\|^2 \geq 0 \) for all \( t \), the expression

    \[ \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 - 2t \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle + t^2 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 \geq 0 \]

    must hold for all \( t \). This is a quadratic inequality in \( t \), which implies that its discriminant must be less than or equal to zero for the inequality to hold universally.

  4. Calculate the discriminant:
    The discriminant of the quadratic \( \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 t^2 - 2 \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle t + \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 \) is:

    \[ \Delta = (-2 \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle)^2 - 4 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 = 4 \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle^2 - 4 \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2. \]

    Simplifying, we find:

    \[ \Delta = 4 \left( \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle^2 - \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 \right). \]

    For the inequality to hold for all \( t \), we require \( \Delta \leq 0 \), which implies:

    \[ \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle^2 \leq \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 \|\mathbf{b}\|^2. \]

  5. Take square roots:
    Taking the square root of both sides (and noting that both sides are non-negative), we arrive at the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

    \[ |\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\|. \]

This completes the proof of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.

Independence and Uncorrelation

1. Independence

Two random variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the occurrence of the other. Formally, \( X \) and \( Y \) are independent if:

\[ P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y) = P(X = x) \cdot P(Y = y) \]

for all values of \( x \) and \( y \). In terms of their joint probability distribution, \( X \) and \( Y \) are independent if their joint distribution factorizes as the product of their individual (marginal) distributions:

\[ f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y). \]

Interpretation: Independence implies a complete lack of association between \( X \) and \( Y \), meaning that knowing the value of \( X \) gives no information about the value of \( Y \), and vice versa.

2. Uncorrelation

Two random variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are uncorrelated if their covariance is zero:

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y])] = 0. \]

Since the covariance is the expected value of the product of deviations from their means, a covariance of zero indicates that \( X \) and \( Y \) do not have a linear relationship.

Conceptual Differences

  • Strength of Condition: Independence implies no relationship of any kind, while uncorrelation only implies no linear relationship.
  • Implication: Independence implies uncorrelation, but uncorrelation does not imply independence.
  • Measurement: Independence is determined by verifying the factorization of the joint distribution, while uncorrelation is quantified by the covariance or correlation coefficient.

Example

Consider a random variable \( X \) that is uniformly distributed over \([-1, 1]\), and let \( Y = X^2 \). Here:

  • \( X \) and \( Y \) are uncorrelated because \( \operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) = 0 \).
  • However, \( X \) and \( Y \) are not independent because knowing the value of \( Y \) provides information about the magnitude of \( X \).
